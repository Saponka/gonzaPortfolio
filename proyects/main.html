EoR and local HII Evidence of the escape of ionizing photons from young compact HII regions

[InterstellarMedium - Star-forming regions](Collaboration with ASTRO-3D)




The ionizing photons of the stars that populated the first galaxies contributed to the progressive reionization of hydrogen from the early Universe.
This cosmic process took place during the period known as Epoch of Reionization (EoR) of hydrogen.
At the end of the EoR, all the hydrogen in the intergalactic medium (IGM) was ionized and has remained in this atomic state for the last ~13 billion years.
The EoR modified the physical state of the IGM. As a result, during that time galaxies grew and formed under conditions different from the current ones.
For this reason and for the cosmological importance of the EoR, understanding the cosmic process of reionization is one of the main objectives of modern astrophysics.
One of the main unknowns is the number of ionizing photons that manage to escape from the galaxies during the EoR, which is usually expressed in terms of "escape fraction" of photons from the Lyman series continuum of hydrogen (LyC).
In this project, we study three compact HII regions of the Small Milky Cloud (SMC) with integrated field spectroscopy (IFS) obtained with GMOS-S at Gemini South.
The goals are: to obtain 3D maps and emission line ratios, to evaluate the effect of the geometry and the structure of each HII region in the escape of ionizing photons, and to infer properties such as metallicity and ionization state with the spatial resolution provided by the IFS technique.

NISCAL

[Observation Techniques]




NISCAL optimizes telluric correction and flux calibration, minimizing the contribution of the stellar spectrum to the telluric correction, automating the search for the best available stellar template, and tracking errors to estimate the level of uncertainty in the final spectrum.
The current version is for slit spectroscopy of point sources and is being developed for:
LS spectroscopy of extended objects, IFU spectroscopy, MOS, and flux calibration of multi-epoch data.

The Starburst-AGN connection

[Variability of NGC 2992]-[Nuclear star formation in NGC 253]-[Nuclear cinematics of NGC 5128]




Increasing evidence suggests that there are potential links between the properties of active galactic nuclei (AGNs), their host galaxies and the parent dark matter halos.
Current models suggest that Super Massive Black Holes (SMBH) in the center of galaxies acquire most of their mass through accretion of the surrounding material.
However, the physics of AGNs is an open debate because there is no coherent scenario for the growth mechanisms of a SMBH that explains the connection with the growth of the host galaxy.
In this project, we study the spectroscopic and photometric properties of a sample of active and inactive galaxies, of all types, in the optical and in the infrared, from spatial scales of tens of parsecs to the largest interactions.
The goal is to explore the connection between star formation and nuclear activity, a problem historically known as the starburst-AGN connection.

Star Clusters in the LMC

[Stellar Astrophysics]




Star clusters are excellent targets to determine stellar ages, metallicities, radial velocities and reddening.
This information is required to test star formation and chemical histories of their host galaxies, and can be obtained from integrated spectrum fitting techniques.
In this project, we study stellar clusters in the Large and Small Milky Clouds, which are two close satellite galaxies of our Milky Way Galaxy.

Low Ionization Systems in Planetary Nebulae

[Insterstella Medium - PNe]




Planetary nebulae (PNe) mark the final stage of evolution for low-to-intermediate mass stars, showcasing a complex interplay of ionized and molecular gases.
During the red-giant and asymptotic giant branch phases, stars expel a significant portion of their mass, which becomes ionized by the ultraviolet radiation from a hot central star.
Interestingly, a fraction of PNe also contains molecular gas, such as H2 and CO, with emission mechanisms ranging from collisions to UV fluorescence.
Recent discoveries have detected H2 transitions in high-velocity low-ionization structures (LISs) within PNe like K 4-47, NGC 7662, and NGC 7009, prompting crucial questions about their formation and survival under intense UV radiation.
To delve deeper into these phenomena, our project utilizes the Gemini Near-Infrared Spectrograph (GNIRS) at the Gemini-North telescope to conduct near-infrared long-slit spectroscopy on PNe NGC 6818 and NGC 7354.

This research will enhance our understanding of stellar evolution, mass loss, clump formation, and the intricate processes involved in the formation of planetary nebulae.
