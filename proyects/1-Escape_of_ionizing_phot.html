Escape of ionizing photons




In this project, I'm studying the internal structures of two compact HII regions with high spatial resolution, using IFU spectroscopy obtained with GMOS at Gemini South over a wide spectral range.
Since it is not possible to resolve individual HII regions in galaxies during the EoR, we turned to the closest local analogue:
the Small Magellanic Cloud (SMC). The sample includes SMC N88A (R.A.=01h 24m 8.3s, Dec.=-73¬∞ 09' 04", V=12.18 mag) and SMC N81 (R.A.=01h 09m 12.94s, Dec.=-73¬∞,11' 39", V=11.7 mag). Both regions where selected for their brightness and size, and a low number of known hosted stars. The long-term goal is to improve our interpretation of nebular emission line ratios observed in EoR galaxies, and their connection to fesc. In particular, the primary objectives are:
1) To determine the effect of geometry and gas structure on the escape of ionizing photons from individual HII regions, and 2) To test the relative stability of the indicator S32 ([SIII](λ 9069+ λ 9531)/[SII](λλ 6717,6731)) with respect to O32 ([OIII](λ 5007)/[OII](λλ 3727,3729)), as predicted by the models used in the interpretation of observations of high-z galaxies. We are building 3D maps of emission line ratios to evaluate the effect of the geometry and the structure of each HII region in the escape of ionizing photons, and to infer properties such as metallicity and ionization state with the spatial resolution provided by the IFS technique.
The specific objectives from the line ratio maps are:
1) To identify the level of spatial correlation in the maps of the O32 and S32 ratios.
2) To calculate the ionization parameter according to the indicators O32 and S32.
3) To calculate the integrated line ratios over the entire HII region and compare with measurements on spatially resolved elements within the HII region.
4) To calculate fesc from the ratios of integrated lines and the support of theoretical models.
5) To determine if the integrated values are representative of the entire HII region.
6) To identify areas with high fesc probabilities within each observed HII region and analyze their dependence on the theoretical model adopted. The main activity during the first stage has been data reduction. The data set is composed of twelve positions in SMC-N81 and six positions in SMC-N88A, with two wavelength configurations in each position (blue and red), and three exposures in each configuration. As a result we have 108 science data cubes, plus two cubes of the standard flux star and six cubes of two telluric stars, making a total of 116 data cubes to reduce.

Top Row: False color images of our compact HII region sample observed with WFPC2 on HST created by combining F656n (H-alpha, red), F502n ([OIII], green), and F487n (H-beta, blue). Light blue rectangles represent IFU-R tiles of 5"x3.5" with each image showing the rough mosaic positions of our proposed observations. The number of tiles varies depending on the size on-sky of each target and we allow a 0.5" superposition of each pointing. Full spatial coverage of each nebula is very important in order to measure total fesc from H-beta emission. Bottom Row: False color images of each nebula using the same WFPC2 data that has been blurred to match the typical seeing at Gemini South using IRAF psfmatch. We have also resampled to a 0.2" pixelscale to roughly match the diameter of the hexagonal fibers used for GMOS-IFS. Significant structure will be resolved in our GMOS-IFS observations allowing for reliable comparison to photoionization models.
