Brief career summary

My name is Gonzalo and I'm a professional astronomer with a strong background in optical and infrared astronomical observations. I completed the Degree in Astronomy (Lic.) of the National University of San Juan, Argentina, in 2009, and I obtained my PhD in Astrophysics from Swinburne University of Technology, Australia, in 2015. I continued my research as a postdoc in CONICET (Argentina), and later as a Science Fellow of the Gemini Observatory from 2017 to 2020. In this period I gained a deep understanding of observatory operations and a lot of experience with optical and infrared astronomical observations in all modes. I also gained experience with near-infrared instrumentation support. After that, I returned to Argentina to work at the Astronomical Observatory of Córdoba, of the National University of Córdoba. I currently hold the position of "Associate Researcher" in CONICET.

Interest in Science

I have a broad interest in science, and I find all astronomy topics fascinating. As a result, I've been involved in widely different scientific research since very early in my career. I started working on Planetary Science running N-body simulations of the solar system in 2006/2007 (paper link), but I quickly realized my passion for astronomical observations, so I shifted to Stellar Astrophysics by the end of my Degree. My undergrad thesis was an IRAF procedure to measure stellar rotation based on the power spectrum (Fourier Transform) of the cross-correlation function between the observed spectrum and a template (paper link). However, the topic in which I have contributed the most is the Epoch of Re-ionization of hydrogen, with observations of high redshift galaxies and metal absorption systems in their circum-galactic medium (papers). This is a very complex topic that requires understanding of physical processes at all cosmic scales, from the sub-parsec scales of star-forming regions, to the Mega-parsec scales of the intergalactic medium radiative transfer. It also requires very challenging observations that push 8 and 10 meter telescopes to their limits.






Epoch of Re-ionization and escape of ionizing photons (EoR/LyC)

Currently, my main interest is the escape of ionizing photons from young compact HII regions in dwarf galaxies. The so-called "escape fraction" of ionizing photons is a key parameter in models of re-ionization. However, this radiation cannot be measured directly in the galaxies that re-ionized the Universe at redshift z > 6, simply because it is absorbed by neutral hydrogen of the intergalactic medium in the same process of cosmic re-ionization. Therefore, the astronomical community is focused in finding an alternative tracer that allows us study this phenomenon directly in the re-ionization epoch with the next generation of telescopes.




Active Galactic Nuclei. (Dusty AGNs)

I'm also interested in the feeding processes of Active Galactic Nuclei. Despite several decades of research, we still lack a coherent scenario for the growth mechanisms of a Supermassive Black Hole, and the connection between the growth of the Black Hole and the host galaxy. In this area, I'm part of a collaboration which recently received funding to study the strong correlations between the mass of the central Black Holes and various properties of the galaxies in which they reside, such as the stellar velocity dispersion of the bulge, its mass and its luminosity. The goal is to explore possible evolutionary paths between: violent ignition and quenching of star formation, ignition of nuclear activity, and morphological transformation of the host galaxy.

Observation

Finally, I've always been interested in the process of observation and measurement of astronomical sources, regardless their nature. In this area, I'm currently working on a method to improve telluric absorption correction and flux calibration, in near-infrared spectroscopy. I've been developing a Python based tool to optimize the telluric correction obtained from comparison stars (also called "telluric" stars). The current version is for long-slit observations of point sources, and I'm planning to extend it to multi-object and integral field modes of spectroscopy.
